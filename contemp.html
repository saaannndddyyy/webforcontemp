The Concept of Society

Civil society, a concept central to social theory since the 1980s, has been seen as essential for democratization in communist regimes like China and liberal democracies. Civil society, as conceptualized by Hegel and Marx, is distinct from the state and economy, focusing on religious, educational, cultural, and professional bodies, interest groups, and campaigning groups. It also includes informal links between neighbors and hobby enthusiasts.

Campaigning for Human Rights

Cosmopolitanism emphasizes universal human equality and rights, with transnational organizations like Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International monitoring and protesting abuses worldwide. These organizations, based in the USA, demonstrate transnational action to protect individual rights and promote global citizenship.

Women's Rights and Transnational Solidarity

The 1970s saw a surge in transnational networks and organizations, with women's groups like the International Council of Women and International Women's Suffrage Alliance advocating for women's rights in international law. Latin American feminists played a crucial role in promoting women's representation within the UN. Post-1970s feminist groups focused on specific issues, rejecting centralized structures for more flexible networks.

Green Activists

Green activists protect the natural world from destruction and pollution through lobbying, protests, and scientific research. They represent global civil society and world citizenship, with environmental groups like Friends of the Earth and the World Wildlife Fund contributing significantly.

Food Security

The concept of “Food Security” is describe as the way in which food is always produced in an adequate amount and also easily accessible to the population while also accounting for the continuous growth of the population and also possible changes or fluctuations in prices or production.

Critiques

The current view also has flaws that critics have pointed out. The most severe and devastating critique is the fact that the current concept primarily focuses on trade-based approaches in dealing with the issue, disregarding or ignoring social and political contexts.

Stability

The term "stability" in an economic context conveys the notions of firmness, permanence, and resistance to disruptive change. The International Monetary Fund (IMF) defines economic stability as avoiding large swings in economic activity, high inflation, and excessive volatility in financial markets. Historically, economic patterns, including cycles of rapid growth and subsequent depression, have been recognized since the analysis of economists like Sismondi and Schumpeter.

Sustainability

is distinct from stability, focusing on the long-term capacity of a system rather than short-term resistance to change. The Bruntland Report (1987) defines sustainability as development meeting present needs without compromising future generations.

Complexity Approach to Markets

A complexity approach to markets advocates a paradigm shift towards holistic, interdisciplinary, and pragmatic solutions to address the challenges posed by the intricate dynamics of modern economies.

Pluralism of Development Models

Post-1980s saw the rise of neoliberal ideology advocating markets as efficient for social coordination and economic growth. Globalization, economic reforms, and collapse of communism fueled the belief that a liberal agenda was the only path for development.
